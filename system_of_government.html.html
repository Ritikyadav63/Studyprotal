System of Government

I. Parliamentary System

India follows the *Parliamentary system* where the executive is responsible to the legislature and derives its authority from it.

Key Features:

  • Collective Responsibility: Article 75(3) – The Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha.
  • President as Nominal Head: Article 74 – There shall be a Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at the head to aid and advise the President.
  • Prime Minister as Real Executive: De facto authority lies with the PM and the cabinet.
  • Vote of No Confidence: If the Lok Sabha passes a no-confidence motion, the government must resign.

Examples:

India, United Kingdom, Japan

II. Presidential System

In the *Presidential system*, the executive is independent of the legislature and holds fixed tenure.

Key Features:

  • President is both Head of State and Government.
  • Fixed term of office and separation of powers.
  • Not accountable to the legislature for day-to-day functioning.

Examples: United States, Brazil, Nigeria

III. Unitary System

A *Unitary system* has a single center of power. The central government holds supreme authority, and local governments operate under its control.

Key Features:

  • No constitutional division of powers between center and units.
  • Local authorities are subordinate.
  • Uniform administration and laws.

Examples: United Kingdom, China, France

IV. Federal System

A *Federal system* constitutionally divides power between the central and state governments. Both operate independently.

Federal Features in India:

  • Dual Polity: Centre and States (Article 1 – India that is Bharat, a Union of States).
  • Distribution of Powers: Article 246 – Three lists: Union, State, Concurrent (Seventh Schedule).
  • Written Constitution: Provides clarity on power distribution.
  • Supremacy of Constitution: All laws must conform to the Constitution.
  • Independent Judiciary: Article 124 – Establishment of Supreme Court; role in federal disputes (Article 131).

Unitary Features in Indian Federalism:

  • Strong Centre: More subjects in Union List, residuary powers with Centre (Article 248).
  • Emergency Powers: Centre can take full control during emergency (Articles 352, 356).
  • Single Constitution and Citizenship: Articles 5–11 (citizenship), single constitution for both Centre and States.

India is described as a “quasi-federal” state with a strong unitary bias.

Conclusion

India has adopted a *Parliamentary form* and a *quasi-federal structure* with unitary features to ensure unity, efficient governance, and accommodation of diversity across states. Relevant constitutional provisions ensure a balanced framework.

Making of the Indian Constitution

Introduction

The Constitution of India is the supreme law of the land. Its making was a historic process that involved extensive debates, multiple viewpoints, and democratic deliberation in the Constituent Assembly from 1946 to 1950.

Historical Background

  • Government of India Acts (1909, 1919, 1935): Provided initial frameworks for governance and introduced limited reforms.
  • Indian National Movement: Demands for self-rule and full independence led to calls for a Constituent Assembly.
  • Cabinet Mission Plan (1946): Proposed the formation of the Constituent Assembly.

Formation of the Constituent Assembly

  • Constituted in 1946 under the Cabinet Mission Plan.
  • Total members: 389 (later 299 after partition).
  • Members were indirectly elected by provincial legislatures.
  • Presided over by Dr. Rajendra Prasad (President of Assembly).

Important Personalities

  • Dr. B.R. Ambedkar: Chairman of the Drafting Committee, known as the Architect of the Constitution.
  • Jawaharlal Nehru: Presented the “Objectives Resolution”.
  • Sardar Patel, Maulana Azad, K.M. Munshi, Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar: Prominent members and key contributors.

Objectives Resolution (1946)

Introduced by Nehru, it laid the philosophical foundation of the Constitution:

  • India to be an independent sovereign republic.
  • Justice, liberty, equality for all citizens.
  • Protection of minorities and backward sections.

Key Committees of the Constituent Assembly

  • Drafting Committee: Headed by Ambedkar – prepared the final draft.
  • Union Powers Committee: Chaired by Nehru – decided the division of powers.
  • Advisory Committee on Fundamental Rights and Minorities: Chaired by Sardar Patel.
  • Multiple other specialized committees on language, provinces, judiciary, etc.

Debates and Deliberations

  • The Assembly met for 11 sessions over 2 years, 11 months, and 18 days.
  • Over 7,600 amendments were considered; around 2,500 were accepted.
  • Discussions were held in a democratic, inclusive, and transparent manner.

Adoption of the Constitution

  • Adopted on 26 November 1949.
  • Came into effect on 26 January 1950 (celebrated as Republic Day).
  • Declared India a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic.

Salient Features of the Indian Constitution

  • Longest written constitution in the world.
  • Blend of rigidity and flexibility.
  • Federal structure with unitary features.
  • Parliamentary system of government.
  • Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles.
  • Secular and inclusive framework.

Conclusion

The making of the Indian Constitution was a monumental task achieved through collective effort and vision. It reflects the aspirations of a newly independent nation and continues to guide India’s democratic journey.

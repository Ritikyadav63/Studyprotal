Fundamental Rights, Directive Principles of State Policy & Fundamental Duties

1. Fundamental Rights (Part III, Articles 12–35)

Fundamental Rights are essential for the development of individuals and democracy. They are enforceable by the courts and protect citizens from arbitrary actions of the state.

Types of Fundamental Rights:

  1. Right to Equality (Articles 14–18): Equality before the law, prohibition of discrimination, and abolition of untouchability and titles.
  2. Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22): Freedom of speech, assembly, association, movement, residence, and profession; protection in respect of conviction and arrest.
  3. Right against Exploitation (Articles 23–24): Prohibition of human trafficking and child labor.
  4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28): Freedom of conscience and practice, profession, and propagation of religion.
  5. Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30): Protection of interests of minorities and right to establish and administer educational institutions.
  6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32): Right to approach the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights (called the "heart and soul" of the Constitution by Dr. Ambedkar).

2. Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV, Articles 36–51)

DPSPs are guidelines for the state to establish a just society. They are non-justiciable but fundamental in governance.

Key Directives:

  • Promote welfare of the people (Art. 38).
  • Ensure equal pay for equal work (Art. 39).
  • Provide free legal aid (Art. 39A).
  • Promote education and health (Art. 41–47).
  • Improve environment and protect forests (Art. 48A).
  • Organize village panchayats (Art. 40).
  • Promote international peace and security (Art. 51).

Note: Though not enforceable, many DPSPs have been implemented through laws and policies, e.g., Right to Education Act.

3. Fundamental Duties (Part IVA, Article 51A)

Fundamental Duties were added by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976, based on the Soviet model. They act as moral obligations for citizens to uphold national values.

List of Fundamental Duties:

  1. Abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals and institutions.
  2. Cherish noble ideals of freedom struggle.
  3. Uphold sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India.
  4. Defend the country and render national service.
  5. Promote harmony and common brotherhood.
  6. Value and preserve the rich heritage of our culture.
  7. Protect and improve the natural environment.
  8. Develop scientific temper, humanism, and spirit of inquiry.
  9. Safeguard public property and abjure violence.
  10. Strive for excellence in all spheres of activity.
  11. (Added by 86th Amendment) Ensure educational opportunities for children between 6 and 14 years.

Conclusion

Fundamental Rights, DPSPs, and Fundamental Duties together form the core of the Indian constitutional philosophy—balancing individual freedom, state responsibility, and citizen duties for the welfare of society.

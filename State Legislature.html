State Legislature: Legislative Assembly and Legislative Council

The *State Legislature* in India may be *unicameral* (only Legislative Assembly) or *bicameral* (Legislative Assembly and Legislative Council). The provisions related to state legislatures are detailed in *Part VI (Articles 168–212)* of the Indian Constitution.

Article 168: Composition of State Legislatures

Each state shall have a legislature consisting of the Governor and either one house (Legislative Assembly) or two houses (Legislative Assembly and Legislative Council).

1. Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha)

  • Article 170: Composition - Members are directly elected by the people.
  • Strength: Varies by state; minimum 60 and maximum 500 members.
  • Term: 5 years unless dissolved earlier.
  • Presiding Officer: Speaker and Deputy Speaker.

Powers and Functions

  • Has full legislative powers for state subjects (State List and Concurrent List).
  • Can introduce and pass money bills (Article 198).
  • Controls the state executive through no-confidence motions, discussions, and questions.
  • Approves the state budget and financial bills.
  • Participates in electing the President of India.

2. Legislative Council (Vidhan Parishad)

  • Article 171: Composition - Members are partly elected and partly nominated.
  • Tenure: Permanent body; one-third of its members retire every 2 years.
  • Maximum Strength: Cannot exceed one-third of the strength of the Legislative Assembly, and in no case less than 40 members.
  • Presiding Officer: Chairman and Deputy Chairman.

Powers and Functions

  • Acts as a revising chamber; can delay ordinary bills but not reject them.
  • Cannot initiate or amend money bills (Article 198).
  • Can discuss matters of public interest and influence legislation through recommendations.

Comparison: Legislative Assembly vs Legislative Council

  • Legislative Assembly holds more powers, especially financial and executive control.
  • Legislative Council has advisory and revising powers but limited authority.

Creation and Abolition

Article 169: Parliament may create or abolish Legislative Councils in states by law if the Legislative Assembly of the state passes a resolution by a special majority.

Conclusion

The structure of state legislatures reflects federal principles and gives states the flexibility to adopt a unicameral or bicameral system. The Legislative Assembly plays a dominant role in state governance, while the Legislative Council supports with deliberation and expertise.
